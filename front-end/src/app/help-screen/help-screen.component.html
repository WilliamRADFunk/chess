Chess
Pawn Moves 01: A pawn can move one space (the opposite side of the bard from which it started), if not blocked by another piece.

Pawn Moves 02: For its first move, a pawn can move vertically two spaces if it isn't blocked by another piece.

Pawn Moves 03: A pawn can capture an opponent piece only by moving diagonoally one space in its permitted vertical direction.

Knight Moves: Any combination of two spaces vertically and one horizontally, or two horizontally and one vertically. Order doesn't matter.

The knight can "jump over" pieces, but can not "land" on it's own pieces.

Rook Moves 01: The rook moves any number of spaces along a straight line, vertically or horizontally.

Rook Moves 02: A rook is blocked by its own pieces. It can't jump pieces like a knight.

It can capture an enemy piece along those straight lines, but again can't jump pieces like the knight.

Bishop Moves 01: The bishop moves along a diagonal line in any of the four directions from its position.

Bishop Moves 02: A bishop is blocked by its own pieces. It can't jump pieces like a knight.

It can capture an enemy piece along those diagonal lines, but again can't jump pieces like the knight.

Queen Moves: The queen is unique in that it can do what both the bishop and the rook can do.

King Moves 01: The king can move one space in any direction, except when blocked by one of its own pieces.

Note: it can't move into a space that an enemy piece can attack during its upcoming turn.

King Moves 02: If the king hasn't made a move before, and the same goes for one of the rooks, it can perform a maneuver called "castling".

The king moves two spaces toward the rook, and the rook moves to the opposite side of the king from which it started.

Note: the spaces between the king and rook must be empty for this maneuver to work.

Promotion: When a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board, it gets promoted.

You can choose to replace it with a queen, bishop, knight, or a rook by selecting the corresponding option on the left side of the screen.

Checking: When a player's move ends with a piece capable of attacking their opponent's king, that opponent is in check.

That opponent must get their king out of check in their next turn.

Checkmate: If the opponent can't get their king out of check, they lose.

Stalemate 01: If a player is unable to move without putting themselves into check, the game is considered a stalemate.

Stalemate 02: When a player has lost all of their pieces save the king,

they can still manage a stalemate if they survive for 30 turns without going into checkmate.
